## Context (for Paul, not the email)

Paul and Katie met on one of Paul's first nights after moving to London in 2007. Both were students — Paul at King's, living at Commonwealth Hall in Cartwright Gardens; Katie in the neighbouring halls. They met in Cartwright Gardens itself that night — not "in some pub in Bloomsbury" as v1 of this template incorrectly said.

The first-night connection is the hook. Don't overplay it. The tone is: warm, specific, lightly nostalgic, no awkwardness. The shared memory is enough on its own — Paul knows what happened that night, Katie will remember or not, and the email should work either way.

The strategic pivot is onto **London then vs London now**, specifically through a **finance / cost-of-living lens**. This isn't just warmth — it *is* the Paybacker thesis. In 2007 a student could live in central London on a Commonwealth Hall room for under £100/week, energy prices were pre-crisis, mid-contract price hikes weren't a thing, and nobody had heard of a subscription audit. In 2026 a 20-year-old rents a room in Zone 4 for £1,000/month, pays for six streaming services they don't use, and gets a mid-contract broadband rise every autumn. The shift maps one-to-one onto what Paybacker exists to fix.

## Why this version works

- **Opens with the "long shot" framing.** Lowers the stakes, gives her an easy out, makes her more curious not less.
- **Names the exact location** — Cartwright Gardens, Commonwealth Hall. If she was there, she'll know immediately; if she wasn't, it's still a plausible-sounding memory that isn't awkward.
- **"Small group of us… kind of evening that's nineteen years old now and still oddly specific in my memory"** — this is the critical sentence. It's warm, it lightly acknowledges there was something specific about that night, but it puts **nothing** in writing that she'd be uncomfortable with her editor reading. She'll fill in whatever she remembers. If she doesn't remember, she'll still find the line charming. Nothing lost either way.
- **Compliments her work BEFORE mentioning yours.** Genuine, not transactional.
- **The 2007 vs 2026 London pivot** is the actual hook — it ties personal nostalgia to the Paybacker editorial angle. This is exactly the kind of framing a consumer journalist writes columns about. It does half her work for her.
- **The pitch is one sentence.** Doesn't ask for anything specific — gives her room to offer what she can.
- **"Genuinely lovely to find you again anyway"** — low-pressure close. She'll reply even if just to be friendly, which opens the door for Email 2.

## Why Email 2 works

- **Opens with warmth, not business.** "Properly made my week" is true and disarming.
- **Reintroduces the 2007 vs 2026 theme** ("since we were students") so the thesis feels continuous with Email 1 — not a bait-and-switch into a pitch.
- **Puts the FCA / Yapily / ICO line in early** so she knows this is a real regulated tool, not another chancer.
- **Three concrete editorial offers** — not "let's have a call". Journalists respond to specifics.
- **"I'd rather you were honest than flattering"** — lowers her defences, signals confidence in the product.
- **Ends with the coffee line, not the pitch.** Brings it back to the human connection. The pitch is the middle; the frame is still "two people who met in 2007."
